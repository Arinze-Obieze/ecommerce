Mobile Product Detail Page UX Redesign Report

A focused research and implementation report covering the mobile product detail page information architecture, CTA priority, section navigation, content disclosure model, and trust presentation.

Prepared by: Arinze Obieze
Date: April 6, 2026
Scope: Mobile PDP layout, content hierarchy, section navigation, sticky buy behavior, reviews visibility, and implementation

Executive Summary

I redesigned the product detail page around a simple mobile-commerce principle: the page should help the buyer decide and act before it asks them to process secondary content. The prior version had a clean visual style, but on mobile it behaved more like a long informational document than a focused buying surface. Too many cards, support blocks, and secondary modules competed with the image, variant selection, and add-to-cart path.

The revised model reduces the density of the hero area, keeps the purchase controls dominant, replaces the mobile tab pattern with a sticky section-jump rail plus vertically collapsed content sections, and introduces a sticky mobile buy bar so the primary action remains within reach. On desktop, the page retains a richer tabbed presentation, but the mobile experience is now driven by progressive disclosure and faster access to the sections shoppers care about most.

1. What I Changed

  1. Removed the duplicate wishlist and share action cluster so the product header has a single action group.
  2. Reduced mobile hero density by removing the early “What buyers need to know” and large seller card stack from the purchase area.
  3. Kept the purchase-critical sequence tighter: image, title, price, stock, variants, quantity, add to cart.
  4. Introduced a sticky mobile section-jump rail with shortcuts for Overview, Specs, Reviews, and Delivery.
  5. Replaced the mobile tabs model with vertically collapsed accordion sections.
  6. Converted the desktop tab model from “Description-first” to an “Overview-first” structure.
  7. Moved richer seller, trust, and quick-specification content into the Overview section where it is visible but not obstructive.
  8. Collapsed the mobile review form behind a dedicated “Write a Review” control instead of exposing the full form immediately.
  9. Added a sticky mobile buy bar with price, selected variant summary, and add-to-cart access.
  10. Added extra bottom spacing so related content and page termination are not obscured by the sticky mobile CTA.

2. Core Product Principle

A mobile PDP should prioritize the buyer’s decision path first and reveal supporting information at the moment it becomes useful, rather than presenting all product content at equal visual weight from the start.

3. Research That Drove the Redesign

3.1 Baymard: avoid horizontal tabs for core product content

Baymard’s product-page research argues against relying on horizontal tabs to contain core content because tabs frequently conceal important information and reduce discoverability on product pages. This is especially problematic on mobile, where viewport width is constrained and tab labels compete for space.

3.2 Baymard: use vertically collapsed sections instead of subpages

Baymard also recommends vertically collapsed sections over separate subpages for PDP details. This pattern keeps users in one continuous context, preserves orientation, and makes the existence of extra content more visible than a hidden tab or page switch.

3.3 Shopify: prioritize key information above the fold and keep CTA access close

Shopify’s 2024 mobile PDP guidance emphasizes two points that map directly to this redesign: purchase-relevant content should be prioritized above the fold, and sticky CTA patterns can reduce friction once the primary buy area scrolls out of view.

The design implication is straightforward: the best mobile pattern here is not “top tabs as the main content container.” It is a hybrid model with a dominant buy area, sticky section shortcuts for navigation, and accordion-style disclosure for secondary content.

4. Current-State Diagnosis

What was strong

  • The page already had a solid visual foundation and strong product image emphasis.
  • Variant selectors were exposed as pills instead of being hidden in dropdowns.
  • Trust, pricing, and reviews were all available in one place.

What was hurting mobile UX

  • The hero area contained too many blocks at near-equal visual priority.
  • Support, seller, trust, and specs competed too early with the buy decision.
  • The tabs section behaved like a content vault rather than a navigable extension of the PDP.
  • The review form was too heavy for first exposure on a mobile PDP.

4.1 The old mobile flow asked users to process too much before acting

On a narrow viewport, the buyer had to move through image media, product identity, trust chips, informational cards, variant controls, quantity, CTA, seller card, tabs, reviews, related products, and a full footer. Even when each component was individually reasonable, the aggregate effect was a long and dense path before the buyer felt fully anchored.

4.2 Secondary content was not weak, but it was mistimed

The issue was not that seller credibility, specifications, policies, or reviews were unimportant. The issue was their timing and weight. On mobile, those modules belong just after the core buying decision, not before it.

5. Redesigned Information Architecture

Layer New Role Why It Is Better
Hero Image, title, price, stock, variants, quantity, CTA Keeps the buyer on the main decision path without distraction.
Sticky section rail Overview, Specs, Reviews, Delivery shortcuts Gives the speed of tabs without hiding content behind a tab system.
Mobile content sections Accordion-based disclosure Preserves orientation and keeps all section categories visible.
Desktop content area Overview-first tabs Retains a richer desktop layout while aligning content labels with the new model.
Sticky mobile buy bar Persistent price and CTA access Reduces the cost of scrolling and helps users act from deeper sections.

6. Implementation Details

6.1 Product hero simplification

I removed the duplicate action row, tightened spacing, and pulled the seller and trust information into a lighter inline summary. This keeps the hero visually coherent and makes the variant and CTA block feel more important.

6.2 New mobile section model

I introduced a sticky section-jump rail that highlights the active section and scrolls the user to the selected content block. The underlying content model is accordion-based, not tab-hidden, so users can see that Overview, Specifications, Reviews, and Delivery content all exist.

6.3 Desktop kept richer, mobile became faster

The desktop version continues to use tabs, but the first tab is now Overview rather than Description, which better matches the new content hierarchy. Mobile uses accordions because that pattern is more discoverable and less cramped on a narrow viewport.

6.4 Reviews became lower-friction

The reviews section still supports the full review form, but on mobile the form is now hidden behind a dedicated “Write a Review” trigger. This removes a large and visually heavy input surface from the default mobile scan path.

6.5 The buy action remains close at all times

I added a sticky mobile buy bar that keeps price and add-to-cart available below the fold. This is one of the highest-impact improvements because it reduces the interaction cost of exploring details before committing.

  • Reduce variant-selection failure and hesitation patterns.
  • Increase engagement with reviews and policies without increasing bounce.
  • Track interaction rate on sticky mobile buy bar and section-jump rail.

It is about sequencing content so that buying intent, product trust, and product understanding reinforce each other instead of competing for the same first-screen attention.
